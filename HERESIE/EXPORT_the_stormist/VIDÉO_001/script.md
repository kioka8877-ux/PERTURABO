## TROPICAL STORM

**[00:00 → 00:38]** Tropical storm. A tropical storm has sustained winds between 39 and 73 miles per hour. This is the stage at which the system receives a name from a predetermined list maintained by the World Meteorological Organization. The naming system was introduced in 1953, initially using only female names. Male names were added in 1979. If a storm causes exceptional damage or death, its name is permanently retired. Since 1954, over 90 names have been retired from the Atlantic list, including Katrina, Harvey, and Maria.
  *Mots forts: sustained winds, receives a name, permanently retired*

## CATEGORY 1 HURRICANE

**[00:38 → 01:18]** Category 1 hurricane. A Category 1 hurricane has sustained winds between 74 and 95 miles per hour. On the Saffir-Simpson scale, it is classified as causing minimal structural damage to well-built homes. However, the term minimal is misleading. A Category 1 hurricane can topple trees, snap power lines, and leave entire regions without electricity for days. Hurricane Dolly in 2008 made landfall in Texas as a Category 1 and still caused over 1.5 billion dollars in damage. No hurricane should be taken lightly because of its category number.
  *Mots forts: minimal is misleading, 1.5 billion dollars*

## CATEGORY 3 HURRICANE

**[01:18 → 02:01]** Category 3 hurricane. Category 3 is the threshold for what meteorologists classify as a major hurricane. Sustained winds range from 111 to 129 miles per hour. At this intensity, well-built homes can suffer structural damage including the removal of roof decking and gable ends. Trees are snapped or uprooted, and electricity and water may be unavailable for weeks after the storm passes. Hurricane Katrina made its final landfall near the Louisiana-Mississippi border as a Category 3 in 2005. Despite weakening from its peak intensity, it produced a storm surge exceeding 25 feet and caused over 125 billion dollars in damage, making it one of the costliest natural disasters in human history.
  *Mots forts: major hurricane, 125 billion dollars, costliest natural disasters*

## CATEGORY 5 HURRICANE

**[02:01 → 02:54]** Category 5 hurricane. Category 5 is the highest classification on the Saffir-Simpson scale, with sustained winds of 157 miles per hour or greater. At this intensity, a high percentage of homes will be destroyed, with total roof failure and wall collapse. The affected area may be uninhabitable for weeks or months. In the entire recorded history of Atlantic hurricanes, only four Category 5 storms have made landfall in the continental United States. The Labor Day hurricane of 1935, Hurricane Camille in 1969, Hurricane Andrew in 1992, and Hurricane Michael in 2018. Hurricane Irma in 2017 maintained Category 5 intensity with winds of 185 miles per hour for 37 consecutive hours, the longest any cyclone on Earth has ever sustained that wind speed. A single hurricane releases the energy equivalent of roughly 10,000 nuclear bombs per day, most of it through the formation of rain.
  *Mots forts: only four, 185 miles per hour, 10,000 nuclear bombs*

## SUPER TYPHOON

**[02:54 → 03:37]** Super typhoon. A super typhoon is a typhoon with sustained winds of 150 miles per hour or greater, equivalent to a strong Category 4 or Category 5 hurricane. Typhoon Haiyan struck the Philippines in 2013 with sustained winds of 195 miles per hour, one of the strongest landfalls ever recorded. It killed over 6,300 people and displaced 4 million. Typhoon Tip in 1979 holds the record for the largest tropical cyclone ever measured, with a circulation spanning 1,380 miles in diameter. That is wider than the distance from New York City to Houston.
  *Mots forts: 195 miles per hour, 1,380 miles, New York City to Houston*

## CYCLONE

**[03:37 → 04:09]** Cyclone. In the Indian Ocean and South Pacific, the same type of storm is called a cyclone. The physics are identical, but cyclones south of the equator rotate clockwise, opposite to hurricanes and typhoons in the northern hemisphere. This is caused by the Coriolis effect. The deadliest tropical cyclone in recorded history was the Bhola Cyclone, which struck Bangladesh in 1970 and killed an estimated 300,000 to 500,000 people, making it one of the single deadliest natural disasters in human history.
  *Mots forts: Coriolis effect, 300,000 to 500,000, deadliest natural disasters*

## BOMB CYCLONE

**[04:09 → 04:44]** Bomb cyclone. A bomb cyclone, formally known as explosive cyclogenesis, occurs when a storm's central pressure drops by at least 24 millibars in 24 hours. This rapid intensification can turn an ordinary storm into a monster in less than a day. While the term is most commonly applied to extratropical winter storms, tropical cyclones can also undergo explosive intensification. Hurricane Patricia in 2015 intensified from a tropical storm to a Category 5 hurricane with winds of 215 miles per hour in just 24 hours, the most rapid intensification ever recorded in the Western Hemisphere.
  *Mots forts: 24 millibars in 24 hours, 215 miles per hour, most rapid intensification*

## HYPERCANE

**[04:44 → 05:22]** Hypercane. A hypercane is a hypothetical tropical cyclone that has never been observed but is theoretically possible under extreme conditions. First proposed by MIT atmospheric scientist Kerry Emanuel in 1994, a hypercane could form if ocean surface temperatures reached approximately 50 degrees Celsius, roughly 20 degrees warmer than the hottest ocean temperatures recorded today. A hypercane would produce sustained winds exceeding 500 miles per hour and an eye wall reaching into the stratosphere at an altitude of over 20 miles. Some scientists have speculated that hypercanes may have formed in the aftermath of the asteroid impact that killed the dinosaurs, when ocean temperatures near the impact site could have temporarily exceeded the threshold. However, no hypercane has ever been confirmed to exist.
  *Mots forts: 500 miles per hour, killed the dinosaurs, never been confirmed*
